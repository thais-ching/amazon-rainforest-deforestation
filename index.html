About this project

The Amazon Rainforest has faced significant changes due to deforestation and climate change. This website provides an interactive view of how these effects have unfolded over the years.

Discover the effects of deforestation on the Amazon Rainforest over the years. The Amazon Rainforest, commonly called the "Lungs of the Earth," is an immense tropical forest teeming with life and ecological significance, stretching across much of South America. Renowned for its extraordinary biodiversity, it serves as a cornerstone of the planet's ecological balance.

Flowing for nearly 4,000 miles through several countries, the Amazon River acts as the rainforest's lifeline. Its vast basin has created a singular environment that continues to fascinate and inspire people worldwide.

The Amazon Rainforest is facing an unprecedented rate of deforestation, which poses significant challenges to its ecological stability and the global environment. One of the primary drivers of deforestation is agricultural expansion, particularly for cattle ranching and soybean farming. Vast areas of forest are cleared each year to make way for grazing pastures or large-scale monoculture plantations, disrupting the delicate balance of this ecosystem. Logging, both legal and illegal, further accelerates forest loss as high-value hardwoods are harvested, leaving behind degraded landscapes that are more vulnerable to erosion and less capable of supporting native species. This deforestation not only destroys habitats for countless species but also fragments the forest, making it harder for wildlife to thrive and reproduce.

In addition to agriculture and logging, infrastructure projects such as roads and dams contribute significantly to forest loss. Roads open up previously inaccessible areas to logging and settlement, leading to further clearing of the land. Similarly, large-scale dam projects flood vast swathes of forest, displacing indigenous communities and altering local ecosystems. The consequences of deforestation extend beyond the Amazon itself; as trees are removed, their ability to sequester carbon diminishes, exacerbating climate change. Furthermore, deforestation disrupts the water cycle, reducing rainfall and increasing the risk of droughts both locally and globally. Combined, these challenges threaten not only the Amazon’s unique biodiversity but also its role as a critical regulator of the Earth’s climate and water systems.
